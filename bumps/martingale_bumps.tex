\section{Introduction}

The consensus-coupled SGD framework of the companion paper proposes that
distributed learners should share discoveries through a soft consensus
field: each worker is weakly attracted toward the grand average of the
ensemble. This mechanism accelerates feature discovery by making one
worker's bump detection contagious to the rest. However, it has a
fundamental flaw: the consensus pull is always attractive. Every worker is
drawn toward the mean regardless of whether the mean contains any useful
structure. If enough workers happen to cluster near an arbitrary point in
the landscape, the attraction can amplify that cluster into a false
consensus. Workers coalesce not because they have discovered a bump, but
simply because they have attracted each other.

In this paper we propose a modification that eliminates this failure mode.
Instead of pulling every worker toward every other, we assign each pair a
random sign: half of the interactions are attractive and half are
repulsive. If worker $j$ is attracted to worker $i$, it receives a force
toward $i$'s position. If worker $j$ is repelled by worker $i$, it
receives a force away from $i$'s position. The signs are fixed at
initialization and balanced: for each worker $i$, exactly half of the
other workers are attracted to it and half are repelled. The expected
force on any worker from the ensemble is therefore zero. This is the
martingale property.

The consequence is simple but powerful. In empty regions of the landscape
where no bump is present, the attractive and repulsive forces cancel.
Workers diffuse freely, as if uncoupled. But when one worker discovers a
bump and stays near it, the attracted partners drift toward the bump
while the repelled partners drift away. The attracted partners then
discover the bump themselves, creating additional attractors that recruit
still more agents. A genuine bump acts as a nucleation site that breaks
the martingale symmetry through persistent occupation. A random cluster
in empty space, by contrast, generates no persistent occupation: the
worker that created the cluster drifts away, and the forces cancel again.

The system therefore has a built-in filter: it amplifies real structure
and ignores false consensus. Workers coalesce only at bumps, not at
arbitrary points. This is exactly the property missing from the original
consensus-coupled proposal.

\section{Setup}

We use the bump data-generating process from the companion papers. The
regression function $f \colon \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dgp}
  f(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{b_i}{1 + (x - c_i)^\top A_i\,(x - c_i)},
\end{equation}
where each bump has height $b_i$, center $c_i$, and positive
semi-definite shape matrix $A_i$.

We have $n$ agents with positions
$\theta_1, \dots, \theta_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$. At initialization, we draw
a sign matrix $S \in \{-1, 0, +1\}^{n \times n}$ with the following
properties:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $S_{ii} = 0$ (no self-interaction),
  \item For all $i \neq j$, $S_{ij}$ is drawn independently and uniformly from $\{-1, +1\}$.
  \item $S$ is fixed for the duration of the run.
\end{itemize}
Because the signs are fully independent (IID), the expected coupling force on any exploring agent remains zero. 

One consequence of using fully independent coin flips, rather than explicitly balancing each column to have exactly half $+1$ and half $-1$ entries, is that the cascade process is not guaranteed to leave any agents behind. Through a series of re-randomizations, it is possible for every single agent to randomly draw a net positive attraction and be recruited into the bump.

In a low-dimensional setting, this total collapse into a single feature would be catastrophic, as the landscape outside the bump would become a "ghost town" devoid of explorers. However, in our overparameterized regime where the ambient dimension is vast ($d \sim 10^9$ to $10^{12}$) and features are low-rank (e.g., $r = 2$), recruiting the entire ensemble to a discovered bump is actually desirable. The agents converge only in the $r$-dimensional subspace defining the bump. In the remaining $d - r$ orthogonal dimensions, the agents remain functionally independent and free to explore. The vast null space ensures that exploration continues unabated even if the entire swarm is recruited to exploit a specific low-rank feature.

The update rule for agent $i$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:martingale-update}
\theta_i^{t+1}
= \theta_i^t + \eta\,g_i(\theta_i^t)
+ \gamma \sum_{j \neq i} S_{ij}\,(\theta_j^t - \theta_i^t)
+ \xi_i^t,
\end{equation}
where $g_i$ is the local gradient (ascent on $f$), $\gamma > 0$ is the
coupling strength, and $\xi_i^t$ is mini-batch noise.

\section{The Martingale Property}

Consider a single agent $i$ at position $\theta_i$ and suppose the other
agents are distributed without any landscape-induced structure. The
coupling force on agent $i$ is
\[
F_i = \gamma \sum_{j \neq i} S_{ij}\,(\theta_j - \theta_i).
\]
By the balance condition, if the $\theta_j$ are exchangeable (no agent
occupies a privileged position), then $\mathbb{E}[F_i] = 0$. The force
is a martingale: it has zero drift in the absence of asymmetric
structure.

Now suppose agent $j^*$ has discovered a bump and remains persistently
near $c_k$ for many steps. The agents with $S_{ij^*} = +1$ feel a
persistent pull toward $c_k$, while those with $S_{ij^*} = -1$ feel a
persistent push away. But the agents that are pulled toward $c_k$ will
themselves discover the bump, become persistent occupants, and begin
exerting their own attractive forces on a new set of partners. The
repelled agents, meanwhile, are pushed into unexplored territory where
they may find other bumps.

This creates an asymmetric cascade. A single discovery at a bump seeds a
chain reaction among attracted partners, while repelled partners are
dispersed outward to continue exploring. The rate of cascade depends on
the coupling strength $\gamma$ and the bump geometry, but the
qualitative behavior is robust: bumps nucleate clusters, empty space does
not.

\section{Contrast with Consensus Coupling}

In the original consensus-coupled SGD, every agent is attracted to the
grand mean $\bar{\theta}$. The force on agent $i$ is
$-\gamma(\theta_i - \bar{\theta})$, which is always directed toward the
center of mass. This has two failure modes:

\paragraph{False coalescence.}
If a subset of agents happens to cluster in empty space (by stochastic
fluctuation), the resulting shift in $\bar{\theta}$ pulls all other
agents toward that cluster. The cluster grows not because it contains
structure but because the attraction is self-reinforcing. In the
martingale scheme, a random cluster produces both attraction and
repulsion, and the net effect is zero: the cluster dissolves.

\paragraph{Averaging trap.}
When two agents discover two different bumps, the grand mean lies between
them in empty space. Both agents are pulled away from their bumps toward
the void. In the martingale scheme, each discoverer attracts its own
subset of partners directly toward its bump. There is no central average
that pulls everyone to the middle.

The martingale coupling thus solves both problems simultaneously. It
preserves the discovery-propagation mechanism of consensus coupling
(attracted partners find bumps faster) while eliminating the pathological
attraction to empty space.

\section{Simulation}

We test the martingale mechanism on a minimal example: 100 agents in
$\mathbb{R}^2$ with a single bump. Agent~0 starts at the bump center;
the remaining 99 agents are initialized uniformly at random in
$[-50, 50]^2$. The bump is located at $(30, 30)$ with height $b = 5$
and shape $A = 0.5\,I_2$.

We compare two conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Random search}: agents perform independent noisy gradient
    ascent with no inter-agent coupling ($\gamma = 0$).
  \item \textbf{Martingale coupling}: agents perform the same gradient
    ascent but with the signed coupling force from
    equation~\eqref{eq:martingale-update}.
\end{enumerate}
Discovery is defined as any agent besides agent~0 entering within
distance~$2$ of the bump center. We run 200 independent trials for each
condition and record the discovery time.

\section{Why the Martingale Breaks at Bumps}

The martingale property holds exactly only when no agent occupies a
privileged position. A bump breaks this symmetry. An agent sitting on a
bump has a higher ``fitness'' in the sense that its position is
stabilized by the local gradient: the bump's curvature keeps the agent
near the peak even as noise and repulsive forces try to dislodge it.
This persistence is the key asymmetry.

For an agent $j^*$ on a bump, the position $\theta_{j^*} \approx c_k$ is
approximately constant over many steps. The attractive force
$\gamma(\theta_{j^*} - \theta_i) \approx \gamma(c_k - \theta_i)$ on an
attracted partner $i$ is therefore a persistent, coherent drift toward
$c_k$. In contrast, the repulsive force on a repelled partner pushes it
away from $c_k$ but does not push it toward any particular alternative.
The attraction is directed; the repulsion is diffuse.

Over time, this asymmetry compounds. Attracted agents accumulate near the
bump and themselves become persistent attractors for their own partners.
The number of agents near the bump grows super-linearly: each new
arrival seeds its own chain of attracted partners. This is the
nucleation cascade that makes martingale coupling faster than random
search.

\section{Connection to the Spectral Gap}

The analysis of the companion paper showed that the optimal consensus
coupling $\gamma$ should be proportional to the spectral gap of the
irrelevant directions: $\gamma \approx (\log 2)(1 - \lambda_2)$, where
$1 - \lambda_2 = 2\eta\lambda$ for the bump landscape with global
regularizer $\lambda\|x\|^2$.

The same logic applies here, but with a twist. In the consensus scheme,
$\gamma$ must be tuned carefully: too large causes false coalescence, too
small prevents discovery propagation. In the martingale scheme, the false
coalescence failure mode is eliminated, so the upper bound on $\gamma$
is relaxed. One can use a somewhat larger coupling strength without
risking pathological collapse, because the balanced signs prevent
self-reinforcing drift in empty regions. The spectral gap still sets the
lower bound (coupling must be fast enough relative to the mixing time of
irrelevant directions), but the usable range of $\gamma$ is wider.

\section{Multiple Bumps and Exploration--Exploitation}

When the landscape contains multiple bumps, the martingale mechanism
naturally partitions the ensemble. The first agent to find bump~1
recruits its attracted partners; the first agent to find bump~2 recruits
a different set. Because the sign assignments are random and fixed,
different bumps recruit overlapping but distinct subsets of the
population. This provides automatic load balancing: no central
coordinator decides how many agents should explore each bump.

Moreover, the repelled agents are pushed away from known bumps into
unexplored territory. This creates a natural exploration--exploitation
balance. Attracted agents exploit known structure; repelled agents
explore new regions. The balance is controlled by the coupling strength
$\gamma$: stronger coupling means faster exploitation of known bumps but
also faster expulsion of repelled agents into the wilderness, where they
may find new bumps sooner.

\section{Open Questions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item How does the nucleation cascade rate depend on $n$, $d$, and the
    bump geometry?
  \item Should the sign matrix $S$ be symmetric ($S_{ij} = S_{ji}$) or
    asymmetric? Symmetric signs create Newton's-third-law pairs;
    asymmetric signs allow richer interaction patterns.
  \item Can the sign matrix be adapted online based on agent fitness, or
    does fixed assignment suffice?
  \item How does the martingale mechanism interact with the filtered
    center $c_t$ from the consensus paper?
  \item In realistic neural network training, what is the analogue of the
    sign matrix? Can it be implemented efficiently at scale?
  \item What is the optimal ratio of attracted to repelled agents? The
    50/50 split is the simplest martingale, but other ratios may be
    better for specific landscape geometries.
\end{itemize}
