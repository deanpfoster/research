\section{Introduction}

The consensus-coupled SGD framework of the companion paper proposes that
distributed learners should share discoveries through a soft consensus
field: each worker is weakly attracted toward the grand average of the
ensemble. This mechanism accelerates feature discovery by making one
worker's bump detection contagious to the rest. However, it has a
fundamental flaw: the consensus pull is always attractive. Every worker is
drawn toward the mean regardless of whether the mean contains any useful
structure. If enough workers happen to cluster near an arbitrary point in
the landscape, the attraction can amplify that cluster into a false
consensus. Workers coalesce not because they have discovered a bump, but
simply because they have attracted each other.

In this paper we propose a modification that eliminates this failure mode.
Instead of pulling every worker toward every other, we assign each pair a
random sign: half of the interactions are attractive and half are
repulsive. If worker $j$ is attracted to worker $i$, it receives a force
toward $j$'s position. If worker $j$ is repelled by worker $i$, it
receives a force away from $j$'s position. To prevent any deterministic
drift from accumulating, the signs are independently re-randomized at every single optimization step. The expected
force on any worker from the ensemble is therefore strictly zero at all times. This is the
martingale property.

The consequence is simple but powerful. Because the expected force is zero, the coupling no longer pulls agents toward each other. Instead, the coupling acts as a source of purely stochastic \emph{noise} (or effective temperature). In empty regions of the landscape where no bump is present, this injected noise is isotropic and bounded, allowing agents to diffuse freely and stably as if uncoupled. But when agents discover a bump and anchor themselves to it, the variance of the coupling force changes geometry. The anchored agents synthesize a massive, highly anisotropic thermal fluctuation that points exactly along the axis of the discovered bump. The exploring agents are not \emph{pulled} toward the bump; rather, the temperature in the direction of the bump becomes so hot that they are violently boiled over the regularizer's barrier. 

The system therefore has a built-in filter: it amplifies real structure
through directed temperature and ignores false consensus. This preserves the discovery-propagation mechanism while eliminating the pathological
attraction to empty space.

\section{Setup}

We use the bump data-generating process from the companion papers. The
regression function $f \colon \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dgp}
  f(x) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{b_i}{1 + (x - c_i)^\top A_i\,(x - c_i)}
  \;-\; \lambda\,\|x\|^2,
\end{equation}
where for each bump $i = 1, \dots, k$: $b_i \in \mathbb{R}$ is the height,
$c_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the center,
$A_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is a positive semi-definite shape matrix,
and $\lambda > 0$ is a global regularization strength. The
$\lambda\|x\|^2$ term penalizes solutions far from the origin and
ensures $f(x) \to -\infty$ as $\|x\| \to \infty$.

We have $n$ agents with positions
$\theta_1, \dots, \theta_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$. At each time step $t$, we draw
a fresh sign matrix $S^t \in \{-1, 0, +1\}^{n \times n}$ with the following
properties:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $S_{ii}^t = 0$ (no self-interaction),
  \item For all $i \neq j$, $S_{ij}^t$ is drawn independently and uniformly from $\{-1, +1\}$.
\end{itemize}
Because the signs are fully independent (IID) and re-drawn every step, the expected coupling force on any exploring agent is exactly zero. There is no coherent drift.

In a low-dimensional setting, recruiting the entire swarm into a single feature would be catastrophic, as the landscape outside the bump would become a ``ghost town'' devoid of explorers. However, in our overparameterized regime where the ambient dimension is vast ($d \sim 10^9$ to $10^{12}$) and features are low-rank (e.g., $r = 2$), recruiting the entire ensemble to a discovered bump is actually desirable. The agents converge only in the $r$-dimensional subspace defining the bump. In the remaining $d - r$ orthogonal dimensions, the agents remain functionally independent and free to explore. The vast null space ensures that exploration continues unabated even if the entire swarm is recruited to exploit a specific low-rank feature.

The update rule for agent $i$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:martingale-update}
\theta_i^{t+1}
= \theta_i^t + \eta\,g_i(\theta_i^t)
+ \gamma \sum_{j \neq i} S_{ij}^t\,(\theta_j^t - \theta_i^t)
+ \xi_i^t,
\end{equation}
where $g_i$ is the local gradient (as ascent on $f$), $\gamma > 0$ is the
coupling strength, and $\xi_i^t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2D)$ is mini-batch noise.

\section{The Martingale Property and Null-Space Stability}

Consider a single agent $i$ exploring the null space, held near the origin by a global regularizer $\lambda \|\theta\|^2$. The coupling force on agent $i$ is
\[
F_i^t = \gamma \sum_{j \neq i} S_{ij}^t\,(\theta_j^t - \theta_i^t).
\]
Because $S_{ij}^t$ is re-randomized at every step, it is perfectly uncorrelated with the positions $\theta^t$. Therefore, $\mathbb{E}[F_i^t] = 0$. The force
is a strict martingale: it has zero drift. 

Because there is no drift, the martingale coupling acts purely as white noise. If the other agents are wandering near the origin with variance $\sigma^2$, the injected noise $F_i$ has variance proportional to $\gamma^2 n \sigma^2$. As long as $\gamma$ is kept small enough that this injected variance does not overwhelm the restoring force of the regularizer, the null space is unconditionally stable. The agents will never experience a runaway explosion or self-reinforcing false coalescence. They simply experience a slight increase in effective temperature in the null dimensions and continue to diffuse freely.

\section{Contrast with Consensus Coupling}

In the original consensus-coupled SGD, every agent is attracted to the
grand mean $\bar{\theta}$. The force on agent $i$ is
$-\gamma(\theta_i - \bar{\theta})$, which is always directed toward the
center of mass. This has two failure modes:

\paragraph{False coalescence.}
If a subset of agents happens to cluster in empty space (by stochastic
fluctuation), the resulting shift in $\bar{\theta}$ creates a persistent, deterministic drift that pulls all other
agents toward that cluster. The cluster grows not because it contains
structure but because the attraction is self-reinforcing. In the
martingale scheme, a random cluster produces mean-zero noise. Without deterministic drift, the random cluster rapidly dissolves.

\paragraph{Averaging trap.}
When two agents discover two different bumps, the grand mean lies between
them in empty space. Both agents are pulled away from their bumps toward
the void. In the martingale scheme, there is no central average
that pulls everyone to the middle. The forces remain mean-zero and simply increase local variance.

\paragraph{The $1/n^2$ Dilution Failure.}
It is tempting to simplify the pairwise $O(n^2)$ martingale interactions $S_{ij}(\theta_j - \theta_i)$ by assigning each agent a single, agent-wise coin flip $S_i \in \{-1, +1\}$ determining whether they are attracted to or repelled from the grand mean: $F_i = \gamma S_i (\bar{\theta} - \theta_i)$. However, this destroys the algorithm in both regimes. 
First, because $\sum S_i \neq 0$ generically, the center of mass $\bar{\theta}$ receives a massive random kick every step, completely breaking the Newton's Third Law $100\%$ compute efficiency ($1/n$ variance reduction) in the convex null space. 
Second, if Agent 1 finds a bump at $c$ while $n-1$ exploring agents remain near the origin $0$, the grand mean sits at $c/n$. The noise injected into the exploring agents becomes proportional to $\gamma^2 \|c/n\|^2 = \frac{1}{n^2} \gamma^2 \|c\|^2$. The anchored agent's discovery is catastrophically diluted by the $n-1$ explorers, reducing the directional hot plate by a factor of $1/n^2$ and annihilating the exponential speedup. 

The explicit, independent pairwise interactions $S_{ij}$ are fundamentally required to bypass the averaging trap without destroying the convergence of the mean. 

\paragraph{Communication Efficiency: Compression vs. Sparsity.}
While the full pairwise interaction seems to demand $O(n^2)$ dense communication, the algorithm can be scaled efficiently using two distinct paradigms:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Dense Interaction via Hamming Compression:} The swarm can perfectly compute the $O(n)$ dense interactions without $O(n)$ communication by leveraging structured, orthogonal sign matrices (e.g., Walsh-Hadamard). Rather than broadcasting individual parameter vectors, workers broadcast $O(\log n)$ parity sums (linear combinations) of their parameters over a Hamming code basis. Agent $i$ exactly reconstructs its specific $F_i^t$ from these $O(\log n)$ sums. The swarm retains the mathematically pristine $O(1)$ coalescence time of the fully connected graph, while scaling the communication bottleneck to $O(\log n)$.
    \item \textbf{Topological Sparsity ($K \ll n$):} Alternatively, one could permanently fix a sparse interaction graph where each agent only communicates with a small neighborhood of size $K$. Because the injected noise $\gamma^2 K \sigma^2$ drops significantly, the explosion threshold is relaxed by a factor of $n/K$. This permits scaling the coupling strength to $\gamma_{\text{sparse}} \approx \gamma_{\text{dense}} \sqrt{n/K}$, perfectly recovering the exponential speedup! However, the cascade becomes topological: an anchored agent only boils its specific $K$ neighbors over the barrier. The recruitment dynamics transition from global coalescence to a wave of contagion governed by the graph's diameter.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Simulation}

We test the martingale mechanism on a minimal example: 100 agents in
$\mathbb{R}^2$ with a single bump. Agent~0 starts at the bump center;
the remaining 99 agents are initialized uniformly at random in
$[-50, 50]^2$. The bump is located at $(30, 30)$ with height $b = 5$
and shape $A = 0.5\,I_2$.

We compare two conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Random search}: agents perform independent noisy gradient
    ascent with no inter-agent coupling ($\gamma = 0$).
  \item \textbf{Martingale coupling}: agents perform the same gradient
    ascent but with the signed coupling force from
    equation~\eqref{eq:martingale-update}, re-randomizing $S^t$ at every step.
\end{enumerate}
Discovery is defined as any agent besides agent~0 entering within
distance~$2$ of the bump center. We run 200 independent trials for each
condition and record the discovery time.

\section{The Convex Null Space: Multiplicative Noise and Log-Normal Stability}

Before analyzing the escape dynamics in a multimodal landscape, it is instructive to consider the global behavior of the swarm in the convex null space, governed solely by the global regularizer $f(\theta) = \frac{1}{2} \theta^\top H \theta$ (where $H = 2\lambda I$). 

When $n$ agents run independent SGD on a quadratic, they form an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process. The stationary covariance of a single uncoupled agent is $\Sigma_{\text{sgd}} \approx \frac{\eta}{2} H^{-1} C$, where $C$ is the gradient noise covariance \cite{mandt2017stochastic}. For $n$ independent workers, the variance of the ensemble mean $\bar{\theta} = \frac{1}{n} \sum \theta_i$ drops linearly to $\frac{1}{n} \Sigma_{\text{sgd}}$, perfectly simulating a single large batch. If we enforce Newton's Third Law in the sign matrix ($S_{ij}^t = S_{ji}^t$), the sum of all coupling forces is exactly zero ($\sum_{i=1}^n F_i^t = 0$). Consequently, the ensemble mean $\bar{\theta}$ retains this perfect $1/n$ variance reduction; it is completely blind to the martingale coupling.

However, the individual agents $\theta_i$ experience the coupling as injected noise. Crucially, because the force $F_i^t = \gamma \sum S_{ij}^t \theta_j^t$ multiplies the positions of the other agents, the martingale coupling forms a system of stochastic differential equations with \emph{multiplicative noise}. The agents undergo a form of multidimensional Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM).

Standard variance analysis fails for geometric processes because they generate log-normal distributions. If we apply It\^{o}'s Lemma to the squared radius $R_i^2 = \|\theta_i\|^2$ to track the typical (median) trajectory of an agent, we find a drift heavily modified by the It\^{o} correction term. In a 1D slice of the null space, the drift of the logarithm is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}[ d(\ln R_i^2) ] \approx \left( -4\eta\lambda - \gamma^2(n-1) \right) dt.
\end{equation}

Because the coefficient of $dt$ is strictly negative, the typical trajectory is \emph{unconditionally stable}. While the mathematical mean variance of the swarm can explode to infinity (an artifact of the heavy tail of the log-normal distribution), the median agent is constantly pulled inward. The geometric nature of the multiplicative noise physically prevents the runaway feedback loop from happening to the vast majority of the swarm. This implies that we can safely tune $\gamma$ to be extremely large to accelerate search, without requiring an artificial clip radius to save the null space.

\section{Hybrid Stabilization: The Role of First-Order Shrinkage}

While the log-normal median is stable, relying entirely on heavy-tailed multiplicative noise makes the swarm highly jittery. Furthermore, pure noise ($\tau=1$) excels at boiling exploring agents over the regularizer's barrier to discover new bumps, but the same noise makes it difficult for those agents to stably \emph{coalesce} and remain inside the bump once found.

We can solve both the stabilization and coalescence problems simultaneously by reintroducing a very weak, deterministic first-order shrinkage term $\alpha > 0$, pulling each agent toward the grand average $\bar{\theta}$:
\begin{equation}
\theta_i^{t+1} = \theta_i^t - \eta\,g_i(\theta_i^t) + \gamma \sum_{j \neq i} S_{ij}^t\,(\theta_j^t - \theta_i^t) + \alpha (\bar{\theta}^t - \theta_i^t) + \xi_i^t.
\end{equation}
Because the mean $\bar{\theta}$ sits tightly near the origin in the null space, the shrinkage term acts as a stiff artificial regularizer specifically on the agents' deviations from the mean ($-\alpha \theta_i$). This acts as an iron-clad tether, clamping the log-normal tails and converting the system back into a tightly bounded distribution.

More importantly, when a small cluster of agents drops into a distant bump $c$, they drag the grand average $\bar{\theta}$ slightly toward $c$. The shrinkage term $\alpha(\bar{\theta} - \theta_i)$ then provides a deterministic \emph{tractor beam} pulling the remaining exploring agents directly toward the cluster. The martingale coupling provides the massive temperature spike to cross the barrier, and the shrinkage term provides the stabilizing force to lock the agents into the feature.

\section{Asymptotic Case Study: One Million Bumps}

To quantify the massive combinatorial speedup achieved by this hybrid martingale swarm, consider an asymptotic case study. 

Suppose a deep neural network must discover $k = 1,000,000$ distinct low-rank features (1D bumps). All features lie at roughly the same distance $\|c\|$ from the origin, heavily penalized by the global regularizer with energy barrier $E_{\text{reg}} = \lambda \|c\|^2$. We deploy a swarm of $n=100$ agents.

\paragraph{Uncoupled SGD Baseline.}
For an uncoupled agent, the expected time to find a specific bump is $\tau_0 \propto \exp(E_{\text{reg}}/D)$. Let us define one ``round'' of search as the time it takes the $100$ independent agents to randomly discover \emph{one} new bump: $1 \text{ round} \approx \frac{1}{100} \exp(E_{\text{reg}}/D)$.
Because the millions of bumps are effectively orthogonal in high dimensions, the uncoupled agents simply linearly sweep the features (a classic Coupon Collector's problem). Finding all $1,000,000$ bumps requires approximately $1,000,000$ rounds of compute.

\paragraph{The Martingale Swarm.}
We aim to tune the martingale coupling ($\gamma$) and the shrinkage ($\alpha$) to discover and coalesce into all $1,000,000$ bumps $100\times$ faster, finishing the entire search in only $10,000$ rounds.

Because the $n=100$ exploring agents inject isotropic noise into the null space, the effective temperature of the wilderness rises to $D_{\text{null}} \approx D + \frac{1}{2} \gamma^2 n \sigma^2$. To achieve a $100\times$ speedup in discovering unseen features, we simply tune $\gamma$ so the baseline temperature is precisely hot enough to boil agents into new bumps $100\times$ faster: $\exp(E_{\text{reg}} / D_{\text{null}}) = \frac{1}{100} \exp(E_{\text{reg}}/D)$. Because the null space is stabilized by the shrinkage tether $\alpha$, we can safely operate at this elevated temperature without the swarm exploding.

The true magic happens upon discovery. The moment the first agent finds a bump, the temperature specifically along the axis of that bump spikes to $D_{\text{bump}} = D_{\text{null}} + \frac{1}{2}\gamma^2 \|c\|^2$. Because the bump is distant ($\|c\|^2 \gg \sigma^2$), this directional hot plate is astronomically hotter than the null space ($D_{\text{bump}} \gg D_{\text{null}}$). 

Consequently, the time for the rest of the swarm to boil over the barrier and \emph{coalesce} into the discovered bump scales as $\tau_{\text{coalesce}} \propto \exp(E_{\text{reg}} / D_{\text{bump}})$. Because the denominator is massive, this coalescence time drops to $O(1)$ steps---vastly less than a single round. 

Thus, the hybrid martingale swarm solves the high-dimensional search problem perfectly. The isotropic wilderness noise speeds up the discovery of the $1,000,000$ completely unseen bumps by a factor of $n$, reducing the total search from $1,000,000$ rounds to $10,000$ rounds. Simultaneously, the anisotropic directional hot plate guarantees that every discovered feature is instantly shared and exploited by the entire swarm in $O(1)$ time, dynamically compiling the 1D bumps into the high-rank intersections necessary to minimize the global loss.

\section{Anisotropic Temperature and Directional Escape}

To understand why pure white noise accelerates discovery rather than just making the system chaotic, we must examine the covariance matrix of the injected noise.

Suppose we want to speed up exploration by simply increasing the learning rate or injecting more standard SGD noise (raising the effective temperature $D$). In a $d$-dimensional space where $d \sim 10^9$, isotropic noise spreads equally in all directions. The probability that an isotropic random walk happens to take a sequence of steps specifically along the low-dimensional subspace of a distant bump is vanishingly small. Isotropic noise heats up the vast, empty null space just as much as the relevant feature dimensions, worsening the curse of dimensionality.

The martingale coupling entirely avoids this through \emph{anisotropic subspace noise}. The coupling force $F_i^t$ is strictly a linear combination of the agents' current positions. Its behavior depends entirely on where the other agents are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Exploration Phase (Wilderness Clutter):} If all other agents are independently wandering near the origin with small variance $\sigma^2$, the covariance of the injected noise is roughly $\gamma^2 n \sigma^2 I_{n-1}$. The injected noise is isotropic but extremely small, making it harmless in the null space.
  \item \textbf{Exploitation Phase (The Directional Hot Plate):} Suppose $k$ agents discover a bump and become anchored at its center $c$, far from the origin ($\|c\| \gg \sigma$). The coupling force on agent $i$ now contains a massive term $\gamma (\sum_{j \in B} S_{ij}^t) c$. Because the signs are IID coin flips, this sum is a zero-mean scalar multiplier with variance $k$.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting covariance matrix of the noise injected into the exploring agents develops a massive rank-1 spike:
\begin{equation}
  \Sigma_{\text{injected}} = \mathbb{E}[F_i^t (F_i^t)^\top] \approx \gamma^2 k \, (c c^\top) + \text{clutter}.
\end{equation}
The swarm has effectively performed decentralized Principal Component Analysis. By locking $k$ agents into the bump, the ensemble synthesizes a highly anisotropic noise profile that points \emph{exactly} along the axis connecting the origin to the discovered feature. 

From the perspective of Freidlin-Wentzell large deviations theory, the expected time to escape the global regularizer's basin and reach the bump scales exponentially with the barrier height divided by the temperature: $\exp(\Delta U / D_{\text{eff}})$. 
In the orthogonal null dimensions, the temperature remains cold ($D_{\text{eff}} \approx D$). But along the specific 1D axis leading to the bump, the effective temperature becomes massively hot: $D_{\text{eff}} \approx D + \frac{1}{2} \gamma^2 k \|c\|^2$.

The martingale coupling does not lower the barrier, nor does it pull agents via a deterministic tractor beam. Instead, it creates a directional corridor of elevated mobility --- a ``slime trail'' connecting the origin to the discovered bump. Along this trail the effective diffusion coefficient is massively amplified; orthogonal to it the system remains cold and stable.

\subsection{The Thermodynamic Race ($\tau = 1$ limit)}

While injecting pure white noise ($\tau = 1$) provides an exponential speedup for exploring agents, it simultaneously exposes the anchored agents to massive thermal fluctuations. Because the anchored agent is pulled by $n-1$ exploring agents located near the origin, the variance of the force it experiences is $n-1$ times larger than the variance it injects into any single explorer.

This creates a thermodynamic race. For the swarm to succeed, the exploring agents must be boiled out of the regularizer's basin \emph{before} the anchored agent is violently knocked out of the bump.

Let $E_{\text{reg}} = \lambda \|c\|^2$ be the energy barrier of the regularizer. The expected time for an explorer to reach the bump is:
\begin{equation}
  \tau_{\text{explore}} \propto \exp\left( \frac{E_{\text{reg}}}{\frac{1}{2} \gamma^2 \|c\|^2} \right).
\end{equation}
Conversely, if the bump has depth $b$, the expected time for the anchored agent to be knocked out by the $n-1$ explorers is:
\begin{equation}
  \tau_{\text{anchor}} \propto \exp\left( \frac{b}{\frac{1}{2} \gamma^2 (n-1) \|c\|^2} \right).
\end{equation}

For the cascade to grow, we require $\tau_{\text{explore}} \ll \tau_{\text{anchor}}$. Because the coupling parameter $\gamma^2$ cancels identically from both exponents, the viability of the pure-noise ($\tau=1$) martingale search simplifies to a strict, parameter-free structural condition:
\begin{equation}
  b > (n-1) E_{\text{reg}}.
\end{equation}

If a feature is sufficiently deep (its local depth exceeds $n-1$ times the global regularization penalty at that location), the anchored agent is unconditionally stable against the swarm's noise. The martingale coupling can then be safely tuned to maximize the exponential speedup $\tau_{\text{explore}}$, ensuring rapid discovery of the structure without risking runaway explosion in the vast null space.

\subsection{Self-Calibration}

A crucial feature of this mechanism is that it is \emph{self-calibrating}. The injected noise along the axis to a bump is proportional to $\|c\|^2$. Bumps close to the origin produce negligible coupling noise --- but such bumps are easy to find by ordinary SGD, so no assistance is needed. Bumps far from the origin, which are genuinely hard to discover because the regularizer barrier scales as $\lambda \|c\|^2$, produce proportionally stronger directional heating. The coupling automatically concentrates its energy on the problems that actually require help.

This also provides a built-in filter against false consensus. A random cluster of agents near the origin has $\|c\| \approx 0$ and therefore produces no slime trail. The trail only exists when an agent has genuinely ``earned'' a position far from the origin by climbing a real gradient to a real bump.

\subsection{Simulation: Anisotropic Temperature}

We verify this mechanism with a minimal 2-agent simulation in $\mathbb{R}^2$.
Agent~0 is pinned at an anchor position $c = (10, 0)$; agent~1 starts at the
origin and diffuses under the regularizer $-\lambda\|x\|^2$ with SGD noise.
We compare two conditions: uncoupled ($\gamma = 0$) and coupled (martingale
coupling, $\gamma = 0.3$, signs re-randomized every step).

For each condition we project agent~1's trajectory onto the anchor axis
(the direction of $c$) and the orthogonal axis. Theory predicts a variance
boost of $(D_{\text{sgd}} + D_{\text{coupling}}) / D_{\text{sgd}}$ along
the anchor direction and no change orthogonally. With our parameters
($\eta = 0.1$, $\sigma_{\text{sgd}} = 1.0$), this predicts a $10\times$
boost along the anchor axis.







\section{Multiple Bumps and Exploration--Exploitation}

When the landscape contains multiple bumps, the martingale mechanism
naturally partitions the variance. The agents anchored in bump~1 synthesize noise pointing along the axis to bump~1, while agents in bump~2 synthesize noise pointing to bump~2. The total injected covariance matrix becomes a low-rank superposition of the discovered feature directions.

Crucially, because features in $d \sim 10^{12}$ dimensions are overwhelmingly likely to be orthogonal ($c_1 \cdot c_2 \approx 0$), the stabilizing shrinkage tether $\alpha(\bar{\theta} - \theta_i)$ gracefully handles simultaneous discoveries. If agents anchor at $c_1$ and $c_2$, the grand mean shifts to the 2D plane spanned by both bumps. The shrinkage physically pulls the exploring agents toward the low-rank subspace of all discovered features simultaneously, without the forces destructively interfering or tearing the agents apart.

This provides automatic load balancing and an elegant exploration-exploitation tradeoff without any central coordination. The swarm dynamically detects the orthogonal axes of relevant features and selectively turns up the temperature along those specific axes, accelerating the recruitment of exploring agents into the known low-rank subspace while preserving a cold, stable null space for continued search.

\section{Connection to Calibeating and Online Learning}

The mathematical structure of the martingale swarm establishes a profound connection between distributed non-convex optimization and game-theoretic adversarial forecasting, specifically the framework of \emph{calibeating} \cite{foster2023calibeating}.

In the forecasting literature, a procedure ``calibeats'' a reference forecaster if it applies a structured, randomized smoothing kernel (a perturbation) to the reference's output such that the new forecasts are mathematically guaranteed to strictly improve the proper scoring rule without ever doing worse \cite{foster2023calibeating, hart2021calibrated}. Recent work has extended this pure game-theoretic concept directly into Online Convex Optimization (OCO) and multi-armed bandits, demonstrating how online descent methods and multiplicative weights (Hedge) can dynamically construct these calibeating perturbations \cite{lee2022online, chen2026calibeating}.

Our hybrid martingale swarm is precisely a distributed physical manifestation of a calibeating procedure. 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Reference Forecaster:} A swarm of $n$ uncoupled, independent SGD agents achieves an optimal $O(1/n)$ variance reduction in the convex null space, but suffers exponential escape times in the non-convex multi-modal regions.
    \item \textbf{Proper Calibration (Matching the Baseline):} By injecting zero-mean spatial noise via symmetric signs ($S_{ij}^t = S_{ji}^t$), the swarm strictly conserves momentum ($\sum F_i = 0$). The ensemble mean $\bar{\theta}$ is perfectly insulated from the injected noise, guaranteeing that the swarm perfectly matches the optimal $O(1/n)$ convergence rate of the baseline in any purely convex region. It never does worse.
    \item \textbf{Proper Calibeating (Beating the Baseline):} The moment a non-convex feature is discovered, the injected noise geometrically shifts into a massive, directional hot plate (the rank-1 variance spike). The exploring agents exploit this self-synthesized adversarial sequence to boil over the barrier in $O(1)$ time, entirely destroying the baseline's exponential escape time. 
\end{enumerate}

The explicit $\alpha$ shrinkage acts as the multiplicative weight update, gently pulling the probability distribution of the exploring swarm toward the specific orthogonal dimensions (the ``winning arms'') where the bumps reside. The martingale coupling provides a universal guarantee: by exploiting the zero-mean variance of the communication channel, the swarm strictly calibeats standard data-parallel SGD across the entire loss landscape.

\section{Open Questions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item How does the nucleation cascade rate depend on $n$, $d$, and the
    bump geometry?
  \item Should the sign matrix $S$ be symmetric ($S_{ij}^t = S_{ji}^t$) or
    asymmetric? Symmetric signs create Newton's-third-law pairs;
    asymmetric signs allow richer interaction patterns.
  \item Can the sign matrix be adapted online based on agent fitness, or
    does IID assignment suffice?
  \item How does the martingale mechanism interact with the filtered
    center $c_t$ from the consensus paper?
  \item In realistic neural network training, what is the analogue of the
    sign matrix? Can it be implemented efficiently at scale?
\end{itemize}
