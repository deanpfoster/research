\section{Introduction}

Modern neural network training exhibits a striking combination of
regularities. SGD often learns simple structure before complex
structure, whether ``simple'' is defined spectrally, algebraically, or
by feature usefulness
\cite{shah2020simplicity,kalimeris2019sgd,rahaman2019spectral}. At the
same time, the top Hessian eigenvalue typically grows during training
until it reaches the edge-of-stability threshold $2/\eta$, after which
training continues in an oscillatory but stable regime
\cite{cohen2021gradient,arora2022understanding,damian2023selfstabilization}.
The usual explanations for these phenomena focus on the optimizer. In
this paper we ask a different question: how much of this behavior can be
explained by the \emph{geometry of the target function itself}?

Our proposal is that progressive sharpening is a natural consequence of
\emph{progressive discovery}. If the target is a superposition of
features with different intrinsic ranks and different curvatures, then a
fixed-step optimizer should detect the broad, low-rank components first
and only later resolve the narrow, high-rank ones. The optimizer then
appears to move from flat structure to sharp structure, not because it
has an abstract preference for sharpness, but because the accessible
signal changes over time. In this view, the growth of
$\lambda_{\max}(\nabla^2 L)$ during training is the observable trace of a
search process that is uncovering progressively harder features.

To make this idea analyzable, we introduce a family of rational ``bump''
functions on $\mathbb{R}^d$ built from low-rank quadratic forms. Each
component has a center, a height, and a positive semidefinite shape
matrix whose rank is the intrinsic dimension of the feature. Rank-1
bumps behave like ridges, rank-2 bumps like elliptical basins, and
superpositions produce intersections where the effective curvature jumps.
The global regularizer now sits outside the bump sum, so the local
geometry of each feature is separated cleanly from the global envelope:
$A_i$ controls intrinsic structure, while $\lambda$ alone controls how
far the optimizer is rewarded for traveling. This gives a controlled
setting in which feature complexity, sharpness, discoverability, and
global confinement are all explicit and tunable.

The resulting story is simple. In high dimension, a rank-$r$ feature is
hard to find because the informative region occupies only a tiny volume,
and generic moment methods scale like $d^r$. Low-rank structure is
therefore computationally accessible long before higher-rank structure.
Moreover, even if an oracle revealed the exact geometry of all bumps,
assembling the true global optimum would still be a combinatorial
subset-selection problem: one must decide which intersections are worth
pooling and which are too far from the origin to justify their added
height. Once SGD reaches a bump, however, the same geometry determines whether a
fixed learning rate can stably resolve it: broad directions are easy to
follow, while sharp directions push the trajectory to the
edge-of-stability boundary. Progressive sharpening is thus not an extra
phenomenon layered on top of feature learning; it is what feature
learning looks like when the unresolved structure becomes successively
sharper. In the dynamical picture developed later, mini-batch noise
plays the role of an effective temperature: early on it ejects the
optimizer from sharp, high-rank structure but leaves it trapped in
broad, low-rank structure, and as the residual shrinks this temperature
cools enough for sharper features to become stable.

This perspective also clarifies why optimizer choice matters. For plain
SGD, the relevant notion of difficulty is geometric and global: can the
trajectory find the bump at all, and once found can it resolve the local
curvature without becoming unstable? In particular, the benchmark should
not be ``did the optimizer find the exact global maximizer of $f$?''
That objective is both computationally unrealistic and conceptually
misaligned with the regime we care about. The more natural question is
which subsets of low-rank structure are discovered, in what order, and
at what sharpness levels. For Adam, an additional issue
appears: whether the feature geometry is aligned with the optimizer's
coordinate-wise preconditioner. The bump family lets us separate these
effects cleanly, including the possibility that Adam exhibits its own
form of progressive sharpening through an internally shrinking effective
step size as the local signal-to-noise ratio falls.

The rest of the paper develops this picture concretely. We define the
data-generating process and visualize its low-rank components, then
analyze the computational barrier to locating higher-rank bumps in high
dimension. We next connect the model to progressive sharpening,
edge-of-stability behavior, and the contrasting behavior of adaptive
optimizers. The goal is not to claim that neural network loss landscapes
\emph{are} bump functions, but to give a minimal geometric model in
which several widely observed training phenomena arise from a single,
testable story.

\section{Data Generating Process}

We consider the regression function $f \colon \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dgp}
  f(x) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{b_i}{1 + (x - c_i)^\top A_i\,(x - c_i)}
  \;-\; \lambda\,\|x\|^2,
\end{equation}
where for each bump $i = 1, \dots, k$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $b_i \in \mathbb{R}$ is the height,
  \item $c_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the center,
  \item $A_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is a positive semi-definite shape matrix,
\end{itemize}
and $\lambda > 0$ is a global regularization strength.  The
$\lambda\|x\|^2$ term penalizes solutions far from the origin and
ensures $f(x) \to -\infty$ as $\|x\| \to \infty$; its strength can be
annealed during optimization.

\subsection{Rank-1 bumps}

When $A_i$ has rank one, the quadratic form $(x - c_i)^\top A_i\,(x - c_i)$
varies along a single direction. Restricted to that direction the bump is a
univariate rational function; in every orthogonal direction the bump is
constant, decaying only through the global $\lambda\|x\|^2$ regularizer.
The bump is therefore effectively one-dimensional.

\subsection{Intractability of the Global Optimum}

Even if an oracle provided the exact parameters ($b_i, c_i, A_i$) for all $k$ bumps, finding the global maximum of $f(x)$ remains fundamentally intractable.

Consider the overparameterized regime where the ambient dimension $d$ is much larger than the number of features $k$ ($d \gg k$), and assume for simplicity that all bumps are rank-1 (ridges). The peak of the landscape occurs where these ridges intersect constructively to pool their heights.

Because $d \gg k$, the intersection of any subset of these $k$ ridges forms an under-constrained linear system. Finding the point on this intersection closest to the origin (to minimize the $\lambda\|x\|^2$ regularizer) is a simple linear algebra problem, solvable via the Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse in polynomial time ($O(k^2 d)$).

However, the global optimum is not necessarily the intersection of \emph{all} $k$ ridges. The global $\lambda\|x\|^2$ regularizer creates a strict combinatorial trade-off: intersecting all $k$ ridges maximizes the sum of bump heights ($\sum b_i$), but if the ridges are awkwardly aligned, this exact intersection might lie extremely far from the origin, causing the regularization penalty to dominate.

It is often optimal to ignore some bumps. Intersecting a smaller subset of $k' < k$ ridges reduces the collected height in the numerator, but the resulting intersection may be significantly closer to the origin, yielding a higher overall function value. Consequently, finding the global maximum requires evaluating the origin-distance trade-off for every possible subset of the $k$ bumps. For a realistic network learning $k=1000$ distinct features, this poses a $2^{1000}$ combinatorial search. Thus, the global optimization of this landscape is NP-Hard, driven by a feature-selection trade-off rather than the local geometry of the features themselves.

This changes how the model should be interpreted. The point of the bump
landscape is not that SGD is secretly solving an intractable global
search problem. Rather, it is that realistic optimization must proceed
through a sequence of partial discoveries: first find low-rank features
that are geometrically accessible, then decide which combinations are
worth refining, and only much later approach any globally optimal
intersection if one is reachable at all. Progressive sharpening is
therefore naturally paired with \emph{progressive subset selection}: the
learner is not only resolving sharper structure over time, but also
implicitly choosing which discovered features deserve further
coordination. With the regularizer separated from the bump sum, this
trade-off is especially transparent: the bumps determine what structure
exists locally, while $\lambda$ determines how expensive it is to travel
far enough to combine that structure globally.

\section{Finding Bumps in High Dimensions}

When the rank of $A_i$ exceeds roughly five, locating the center $c_i$
from data becomes substantially harder. The core difficulty is geometric:
a rank-$r$ bump concentrates its signal in an $r$-dimensional subspace of
$\mathbb{R}^d$, and the volume of the region where $f$ is appreciably
above the background shrinks exponentially in $r$.

Concretely, consider drawing $x$ uniformly from a ball of radius $R$.
The fraction of samples that land within a distance $\epsilon$ of the
$r$-dimensional ridge scales as $(\epsilon/R)^{d-r}$. For moderate $r$
this fraction is already negligible, meaning most observations carry
almost no information about the bump's location. Standard nonparametric
estimators inherit this curse: their convergence rates depend on the
\emph{intrinsic} dimension $r$, not just the ambient dimension $d$.

This motivates an iterative strategy: first detect the low-rank bumps
(which are easy to find), subtract them, and then search for
progressively higher-rank structure in the residual. Whether such a
strategy can outperform a global fit is one of the central questions
of this paper.

\subsection{The High-Dimensional Computational Barrier}

To understand the severe computational limits of finding these bumps, we must
consider the scale of modern deep learning, where the ambient dimension $d$
(the number of parameters) routinely ranges from millions to trillions.

First, consider naive optimization via Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) from a
random initialization. As established by Statistical Query (SQ) lower bounds,
if the bump is localized, the gradient of the loss is exponentially close to
zero almost everywhere in the domain. The probability mass of the region where
the bump provides a meaningful gradient signal scales roughly as
$(\epsilon/R)^{d-r}$, where $\epsilon$ is the width of the bump in the flat
directions and $R$ is the radius of the initialization region. For $d \sim 10^6$,
this volume fraction is effectively zero. A random initialization will simply
not land in the basin of attraction, and gradient noise will dominate.

To avoid this ``needle in a haystack'' problem, one might turn to spectral or
tensor methods (the Method of Moments) to deterministically find the
$r$-dimensional active subspace. However, these methods require estimating the
$r$-th order moments of the data distribution. The sample complexity and the
computational cost to store and compute an $r$-th order tensor in $d$ dimensions
scale as $O(d^r)$.

When $d = 10^6$ (a relatively small network by modern standards):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Rank-1 ($r=1$):} $O(d) \approx 10^6$. Easily tractable.
  \item \textbf{Rank-2 ($r=2$):} $O(d^2) \approx 10^{12}$. Requiring a trillion
    entries, computing the covariance matrix is already at the boundary of what
    is feasible without extreme sparsity or specialized low-rank approximations.
  \item \textbf{Rank-3 ($r=3$):} $O(d^3) \approx 10^{18}$. This requires
    exascale computing, putting it out of reach for general problems.
  \item \textbf{Rank-5 ($r=5$):} $O(d^5) \approx 10^{30}$. This is a strict
    computational wall. It is physically impossible to store or compute this
    tensor.
\end{itemize}

This severe scaling law dictates that in the overparameterized regime, finding
anything beyond a rank-1 or rank-2 bump via general unsupervised moments is
intractable. Consequently, the optimization process \emph{must} rely on either
strong structural priors within the architecture (which restrict the effective
search space) or iterative, progressive strategies that isolate one low-rank
component at a time.

\section{Parallel Agents and the Geometry of Discovery}
\label{sec:parallel}

A natural question: if one SGD agent progressively discovers bumps from
low-rank to high-rank, do $k$ independent agents discover $k$ times as
many bumps?  In general, no.  The agents redundantly discover the same
easy bumps before any of them finds a hard one.

\paragraph{Basin sizes and codimension.}
The basin of attraction for a rank-$r$ bump in $\mathbb{R}^d$ has
measure that scales as $(\epsilon/R)^{d-r}$, where $\epsilon$ is the
characteristic width of the ridge and $R$ is the radius of the
initialization region.  When several bumps coexist with different ranks,
their basins differ by \emph{exponential} factors.  A rank-1 bump in
$\mathbb{R}^{10}$ has basin measure $\sim (\epsilon/R)^9$; a rank-3
bump in the same space has basin measure $\sim (\epsilon/R)^7$.  The
ratio is $(\epsilon/R)^{-2}$, which for typical $\epsilon/R \ll 1$
means the rank-1 bump dominates overwhelmingly.

Consequently, $k$ independent agents initialized uniformly at random
will almost all land in the basin of the lowest-rank (broadest) bump.
The expected number of \emph{distinct} bumps discovered scales
sublinearly in $k$: for $n$ bumps with basin probabilities $p_1 \gg
p_2 \gg \cdots \gg p_n$, the expected number of distinct discoveries
after $k$ trials is
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}[\text{distinct}] = n - \sum_{i=1}^{n} (1 - p_i)^k
  \approx 1 + \sum_{i=2}^{n} k p_i \quad \text{for } k p_i \ll 1.
  \label{eq:coupon}
\end{equation}
This is far from $k$ when the $p_i$ are highly unequal---precisely the
regime where bumps have a hierarchy of ranks.

\paragraph{When does parallelism help?}
The $k$-agent strategy approaches linear scaling when all basins have
comparable measure, i.e., when $p_i \approx 1/n$ for all $i$.  In our
model this happens when:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{All bumps have similar codimension} ($d - r_i \approx
    \text{const}$).  If every bump is rank-$r$ in $\mathbb{R}^d$, no
    bump has a geometrically privileged basin, and random starts sample
    them roughly uniformly.
  \item \textbf{The ambient dimension $d$ is large relative to the rank
    differences.}  At $d = 3$ with bumps of rank 1, 2, and 3, the basin
    measures scale as $(\epsilon/R)^2$, $(\epsilon/R)^1$, and
    $(\epsilon/R)^0 = O(1)$---a modest hierarchy.  At $d = 100$, a
    rank-1 and rank-3 bump have basins scaling as $(\epsilon/R)^{99}$
    vs.\ $(\epsilon/R)^{97}$---a ratio of only $(\epsilon/R)^{-2}$,
    which is small in relative terms when exponents are already extreme.
    In high $d$, \emph{all} bumps are hard, luck dominates for
    everything, and $k$ agents give roughly $k\times$ coverage.
  \item \textbf{Heights $b_i$ compensate for basin size.}  A high-rank
    bump with a large amplitude $b_i$ has a steeper gradient at its
    boundary, extending its effective basin of attraction beyond the
    geometric ridge width.  When amplitudes are tuned to equalize
    effective basin sizes, parallelism pays off.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Connection to EASGD and GRAWA.}
This analysis explains why naive parallel SGD (and EASGD
\cite{zhang2015easgd}) suffers from redundant discovery: pulling all
workers toward a uniform average compounds the bias toward the dominant
basin.  GRAWA \cite{dimlioglu2024grawa} partially corrects this by
downweighting workers on steep slopes (high gradient norm), effectively
implementing a soft repulsion from already-discovered sharp features.
In the language of our model, GRAWA's gradient-norm weighting gives
higher weight to workers that have settled into a bump (low gradient)
versus those still sliding down toward one (high gradient), encouraging
diversity across basins rather than concentration in the easiest one.

\paragraph{The $d = 3$ regime.}
At $d = 3$ with a mixture of rank-1, 2, and 3 bumps, the basin
hierarchy is mild: codimensions 2, 1, and 0 respectively.  The rank-3
(full-rank) bump has a basin of measure $O(1)$---easy to find by random
search---while the rank-1 bump has codimension 2, making it moderately
hard.  This inverts the usual ordering: the ``sharpest'' bump (full
rank, highest curvature) has the largest basin.  Multiple agents in this
regime \emph{do} benefit from parallelism, because discovery is
dominated by the luck of landing near a narrow ridge rather than by a
deterministic hierarchy.  This is precisely the regime where random
search outperforms gradient-based progressive discovery, and where the
distributed optimization literature's tools become most relevant.

\section{Sharpness and SGD}

Recent work in the optimization of deep neural networks has drawn
attention to the \emph{sharpness} of minima in the loss landscape.
Informally, a minimum is \emph{sharp} if the loss increases rapidly in
some direction, and \emph{flat} if the loss varies slowly in a
neighborhood. Several lines of evidence suggest that stochastic gradient
descent (SGD) implicitly favors flat minima, and that flat minima
generalize better than sharp ones.

\subsection{Definitions of sharpness}

Let $L(\theta)$ denote the training loss at parameters $\theta$, and let
$\theta^*$ be a local minimum. Common measures of sharpness include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Spectral sharpness:} the largest eigenvalue
    $\lambda_{\max}(\nabla^2 L(\theta^*))$ of the Hessian.
  \item \textbf{Trace sharpness:} $\operatorname{tr}(\nabla^2 L(\theta^*))$,
    which captures average curvature across all directions.
  \item \textbf{Worst-case sharpness} \cite{keskar2017large}: the maximum
    increase $\max_{\|\epsilon\| \le \rho} L(\theta^* + \epsilon) - L(\theta^*)$
    over a ball of radius $\rho$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Progressive sharpening}

The \emph{progressive sharpening} phenomenon, documented empirically by
several groups, refers to the observation that the top eigenvalue of the
Hessian tends to increase during training, sometimes reaching the
threshold $2/\eta$ (where $\eta$ is the learning rate) at which point
training enters the \emph{edge of stability} regime.

Our bump model provides a clean setting in which to study this: each
bump in~\eqref{eq:dgp} has curvature controlled by $A_i$ and $b_i$.
A learner that first captures the broad, low-rank bumps and only later
resolves the sharp, high-rank bumps will exhibit progressive sharpening
by construction. This gives a concrete data-generating mechanism for
the phenomenon, rather than relying on implicit properties of the
optimizer.

\subsection{Thermodynamics of Escape (Freidlin-Wentzell)}

To understand \emph{why} SGD resolves broad bumps before sharp ones, we can appeal to Freidlin-Wentzell theory and Large Deviation principles, which model SGD as a stochastic diffusion process.

When SGD falls into a basin of attraction (one of our bumps), the mini-batch noise acts as a thermal bath. The probability of escaping this minimum is governed by the Kramers escape rate $\tau \propto \exp(\Delta V / D)$, where $\Delta V$ is the depth (the ``barrier'' or ``action'' required to escape) and $D \propto \eta \sigma^2$ is the effective temperature (proportional to learning rate and noise variance). Crucially, the pre-factor of this escape time depends strongly on the sharpness (the Hessian eigenvalues). 

If a bump is high-rank and \emph{sharp}, the noise is heavily amplified by the steep walls, and the escape time is exceedingly short. Early in training, when the residual error (and thus the gradient noise $\sigma^2$) is large, the effective temperature is too high for the optimizer to stay trapped in these sharp, high-rank features; it gets rapidly ejected. 

Conversely, if a bump is low-rank and \emph{broad}, the barrier is massive and the curvature is low. The Freidlin-Wentzell escape time is exponentially long. The optimizer gets trapped here first, smoothly descending the broad valley and fitting that component of the data. 

This creates a strict temporal ordering: as the optimizer fits the broad bumps, the residual error shrinks, causing the effective temperature $D$ to cool. This cooling finally allows SGD to stably ``freeze'' into the narrower, sharper basins of the high-rank bumps without being kicked out. This thermodynamic annealing process provides a rigorous physical justification for why we observe progressive sharpening.

\section{Adaptive Optimizers and Coordinate Alignment}

The behavior of adaptive optimizers, particularly Adam, diverges significantly from standard SGD within this framework. Because Adam scales updates inversely by the root-mean-square of recent gradients, its dynamics depend heavily on whether the shape matrices $A_i$ are aligned with the standard coordinate axes.

\subsection{Behavior in Flat Regions}

Far from the bump's center, where the true gradient is vanishingly small, SGD takes a slow random walk driven by mini-batch noise. Adam, however, normalizes this noise. The denominator $\sqrt{v_t}$ shrinks to match the tiny gradient magnitude, causing the effective step size to blow up to the base learning rate $\eta$. Consequently, Adam takes large, rapid jumps ($\approx \pm \eta$ per coordinate) through the flat regions. While this amplified noise might help it stumble upon a bump's basin of attraction faster than SGD, it is essentially performing an undirected, high-temperature search.

\subsection{The Importance of Coordinate Alignment}

Once the optimizer finds the slope of a bump, Adam's efficiency is dictated by the eigenvectors of the shape matrix $A_i$.

\textbf{Unaligned Bumps:} If the bump is not coordinate-aligned (i.e., $A_i$ has significant off-diagonal elements), Adam's diagonal preconditioner fails to capture the true geometry. The update vector, roughly proportional to $\text{sign}(\nabla f)$, forces the optimizer to move along the diagonals of the hypercube. For a narrow, oblique ridge, Adam will violently bounce back and forth across the ridge rather than smoothly ascending it, making it exceptionally poor at resolving oblique high-rank structure.

\textbf{Coordinate-Aligned Bumps:} If the bumps are coordinate-aligned (which is a reasonable model for independent neurons or layers in a neural network), Adam can adapt perfectly to the scale of each coordinate. In this regime, Adam essentially decouples the problem. 

How many such bumps can Adam handle simultaneously? If we have a superposition of $k$ bumps, each aligned to \emph{distinct} coordinate axes, Adam can optimize all of them simultaneously without penalty. Because the updates are computed coordinate-wise, the presence of a sharp bump on axis $x_1$ does not suppress the learning rate for a broad bump on axis $x_2$. However, if multiple bumps overlap within the same coordinate subspace, interference occurs. The running variance $v_t$ for those shared coordinates will be dominated by the gradients of the sharpest feature, potentially hindering the discovery or resolution of overlapping broader structures.

\subsection{Effective Step Size and Noise Annealing}

While modern training regimes often avoid explicit learning rate decay schedules when using adaptive methods, Adam possesses an intrinsic auto-annealing mechanism that replicates the thermodynamics of progressive sharpening.

The update rule for Adam is proportional to the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) of the gradients: $\Delta \theta \approx \eta \cdot \frac{\mathbb{E}[g]}{\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[g^2]}}$ \cite{kingma2014adam}. Early in training, or when traversing the slope of a broad bump, the gradient is consistent across mini-batches (high SNR). The denominator scales with the numerator, resulting in a large effective step size near $\pm \eta$. As discussed in the Freidlin-Wentzell analysis, this large step size combined with high curvature acts as a high effective temperature $D$, violently ejecting Adam from sharp, high-rank bumps.

However, as Adam settles into the broad bumps, the mean gradient (the signal) approaches zero, but the mini-batch variance (the noise) remains. The SNR drops precipitously. Because the denominator $\sqrt{v_t}$ is dominated by this variance, the \emph{effective} step size $\Delta \theta$ automatically shrinks \cite{orvieto2025adam}, even though the global learning rate $\eta$ remains fixed.

This noise-induced annealing is functionally equivalent to decaying the learning rate in SGD. It cools the effective temperature $D$ of the system. Once the broad bumps are fit and the effective step size has decayed sufficiently, Adam can finally enter and stably remain inside the narrower basins of the sharp bumps. Thus, Adam still exhibits progressive sharpening, but the ``decay'' that allows it to resolve sharp features is driven autonomously by the local geometry and noise profile rather than a global schedule.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Optimization benchmarks.}
The functional form~\eqref{eq:dgp} is a direct generalization of the
\emph{Shekel function} \cite{shekel1971test}, a classical benchmark
$f(x) = \sum_i 1/(c_i + \|x - a_i\|^2)$ used to test global
optimizers \cite{jamil2013literature,naser2025review}. The Shekel
function is isotropic ($A_i = I$), has no global decay term, and has
only been used to benchmark evolutionary and swarm algorithms, never as
a data-generating process for studying gradient-based learning dynamics.
The \emph{Vincent function} $f(x) = -\tfrac{1}{n}\sum_i \sin(10\ln x_i)$
\cite{li2013cec}, another multimodal benchmark with many equal-depth
basins, shares the ``bumpy surface'' character but is separable across
coordinates and has no directional ridge structure.
Our model generalizes Shekel in three ways: anisotropic and rank-deficient
shape matrices $A_i$, a separate $\lambda\|x\|^2$ regularizer, and a focus on SGD
dynamics rather than global optimization.

\paragraph{Distributed SGD and landscape exploration.}
Elastic Averaging SGD (EASGD) \cite{zhang2015easgd} maintains multiple
workers exploring a loss surface in parallel, pulled toward a shared center
variable. GRAWA \cite{dimlioglu2024grawa} improves on EASGD by weighting
the center toward workers in flatter regions (lower gradient norm),
explicitly connecting distributed optimization to sharpness-aware learning.
Their visualizations of worker trajectories on the Vincent function---where
multiple agents discover distinct basins before converging---are analogous
to our data-distributed view (Figure~\ref{fig:tworank1data}), with the
key difference that our bumps have directional rank structure rather than
isotropic basins.

\paragraph{Radial basis functions.}
The inverse quadratic kernel $\phi(r) = 1/(1 + \varepsilon^2 r^2)$ is a
standard choice in RBF interpolation
\cite{hardy1971multiquadric,buhmann2003radial}, and recent work studies
data-driven shape parameter selection \cite{hanveiga2024robust}. However,
RBF shape parameters are always scalars (isotropic); the idea of a
matrix-valued, rank-deficient shape parameter controlling the anisotropy
and intrinsic dimensionality of each basis function appears to be new.

\paragraph{Single-index and multi-index models.}
A rank-1 bump, restricted to its active direction, is a single-index
model with a Cauchy-like link function. The \emph{information exponent}
framework \cite{bietti2022single,damian2023multiindex} characterizes the
sample complexity of learning such models, and has been extended to
multi-index settings
\cite{ren2024orthogonal,damian2025generativeleap,bruna2025survey}.
Our bump model provides a concrete parametric family where the
information exponent can be computed exactly as a function of the rank
and eigenvalues of $A_i$.

\paragraph{Staircase property and saddle-to-saddle dynamics.}
Abbe et al.\ \cite{abbe2021staircase,abbe2023leap} showed that SGD
on neural networks learns target functions by sequentially escaping
saddle points of increasing complexity, with the \emph{leap complexity}
governing the time scale. Recent work extends this to general
architectures \cite{zhang2025saddlesimplicity} and deep ReLU networks
\cite{bantzis2025deeprelu}. Our rank hierarchy---low-rank bumps
discovered before high-rank ones---is a concrete geometric instance of
the staircase mechanism, with closed-form Hessians making the saddle
structure analytically tractable.

\paragraph{Progressive sharpening theory.}
Agarwala et al.\ \cite{agarwala2023secondorder} showed that progressive
sharpening and edge-of-stability are generic properties of any model
quadratic in its parameters, not specific to deep networks. Our work is
complementary: rather than showing PS is generic in the \emph{learner},
we construct a \emph{data-generating process} that produces PS by
design through the rank hierarchy of the target function.

\paragraph{Lorentzian peaks in spectroscopy and physics.}
Sums of Lorentzians $1/(1 + ((x-x_0)/\gamma)^2)$ are the standard
model for overlapping resonance peaks \cite{breitwigner1936}, fitted by
Levenberg--Marquardt in one dimension. Our model can be viewed as a
high-dimensional, anisotropic generalization of multi-peak Lorentzian
fitting, but with the very different goal of understanding SGD dynamics
rather than spectral deconvolution.

\section{Empirical SGD Phenomena and the Bump Model}

This appendix surveys well-documented empirical regularities of SGD
training in modern neural networks and assesses which can be replicated
or illuminated by the bump model~\eqref{eq:dgp}. For each phenomenon we
note the key references, the empirical claim, and whether the bump model
offers a tractable analog. Phenomena are grouped by how naturally they
fit the model: \S\ref{sec:direct} (direct analogs),
\S\ref{sec:partial} (partial analogs), and \S\ref{sec:outside}
(outside scope).

\subsection{Direct analogs}\label{sec:direct}

\paragraph{Progressive sharpening and the edge of stability.}
During gradient descent on neural networks, the largest eigenvalue of
the Hessian increases steadily (\emph{progressive sharpening}) until it
reaches $2/\eta$, after which the loss oscillates non-monotonically but
continues to decrease on long timescales (\emph{edge of stability})
\cite{cohen2021gradient}. Theoretical analyses confirm that gradient
descent self-stabilizes at this threshold
\cite{arora2022understanding,damian2023selfstabilization}.

\textbf{Bump analog.} This is the primary motivation of the paper. Each
bump has curvature controlled by $A_i$ and $b_i$, so a learner
discovering bumps in order of increasing rank exhibits progressive
sharpening by construction. Our figures directly show SGD trajectories
confined to the EoS boundary (Figures~\ref{fig:rank1}--\ref{fig:tworank1}).

\paragraph{Simplicity bias and spectral bias.}
Neural networks learn simple features first: linearly separable
patterns before complex ones \cite{shah2020simplicity}, low-degree
polynomials before high-degree \cite{kalimeris2019sgd}, and
low-frequency components before high-frequency
\cite{rahaman2019spectral}. This is sometimes called the
\emph{F-principle}.

\textbf{Bump analog.} Low-rank bumps are the ``simple'' features of our
model: a rank-$r$ bump concentrates its signal in $r$ directions, and
the gradient from a distant rank-$r$ bump is visible in fewer
dimensions. SGD should discover rank-1 bumps before rank-5 bumps, giving
a clean complexity hierarchy indexed by rank.

\paragraph{Implicit regularization toward flat minima.}
SGD with small batches and large learning rates implicitly favors flat
minima \cite{hochreiter1997flat,keskar2017large}. Theoretical work
explains this via the noise structure of SGD: the gradient covariance
penalizes the trace of the Hessian
\cite{blanc2020implicit,damian2021label}, and the effective temperature
$\eta/B$ controls the width of the implicit posterior
\cite{smith2018bayesian}.

\textbf{Bump analog.} Bumps of varying sharpness (eigenvalues of $A_i$)
coexist. With a fixed learning rate, the EoS boundary excludes the
sharpest bumps entirely. One can directly verify whether SGD with
different $\eta/B$ ratios preferentially finds flatter bumps, with
closed-form Hessians providing exact sharpness measurements.

\paragraph{Sharpness reduction at learning rate drops.}
When the learning rate is decreased by a step schedule, the sharpness
$\lambda_{\max}(\nabla^2 L)$ initially drops, then the optimizer finds
a new, sharper minimum within the now-accessible low-loss region
\cite{lewkowycz2020large,cohen2021gradient}.

\textbf{Bump analog.} Reducing $\eta$ raises the EoS threshold to
$2/\eta_{\text{new}}$, expanding the region the optimizer can explore.
Previously inaccessible sharp bumps become resolvable, and the
trajectory should exhibit the drop-then-rise sharpness signature.

\paragraph{Batch size effects.}
The ratio $\eta/B$ is a key control parameter. There is a critical batch
size below which increasing $B$ is compute-efficient and above which
returns diminish \cite{mccandlish2018empirical}. Increasing the batch
size is equivalent to decaying the learning rate
\cite{goyal2017accurate}.

\textbf{Bump analog.} The bump model has analytic gradients, so the
gradient noise covariance can be computed exactly as a function of $B$.
This gives a clean testbed for the critical-batch-size theory.

\paragraph{Learning rate warmup.}
Warmup (gradually increasing $\eta$ from zero) is essential for stable
training of large models \cite{goyal2017accurate}. Without warmup,
large initial learning rates cause instabilities.

\textbf{Bump analog.} Without warmup, a large $\eta$ may catapult the
optimizer past narrow bumps. With warmup, the optimizer first resolves
nearby bumps at low $\eta$ (low EoS threshold), then progressively
accesses sharper bumps as $\eta$ increases. The order of bump discovery
should differ measurably.

\subsection{Partial analogs}\label{sec:partial}

\paragraph{Catapult phase.}
With large learning rates, the loss initially spikes before rapidly
decreasing to a better region than small learning rates would reach
\cite{lewkowycz2020large,gilmer2022loss}. This beneficial instability
helps escape poor initialization basins.

\textbf{Bump analog (partial).} If initialized far from any bump, a
large step could overshoot local structure and land near a more
prominent bump. The effect should be observable but less dramatic than in
deep networks.

\paragraph{Gradient starvation.}
In classification, once one feature becomes sufficiently predictive, the
gradients for other features are suppressed
\cite{pezeshki2021gradient}. This explains why networks fail to learn
redundant features.

\textbf{Bump analog (partial).} Fitting a dominant bump reduces the
residual signal from smaller bumps, suppressing their gradients. The
mechanism differs (signal magnitude vs.\ cross-entropy saturation) but
the qualitative effect---dominant features starve subordinate ones---is
the same.

\paragraph{Double descent.}
Test error follows a U-shaped ``double descent'' curve: it first
decreases, spikes at the interpolation threshold, then decreases again
in the overparameterized regime
\cite{belkin2019reconciling,nakkiran2020deep}.

\textbf{Bump analog (partial).} Requires pairing the bump data with a
learner of varying capacity (e.g., varying the number of basis functions).
At the interpolation threshold the learner must use high-curvature
solutions; overparameterized learners can select flat ones. The bump
model's multi-scale structure should produce a clear threshold effect.

\paragraph{Mode connectivity.}
Distinct trained networks are connected by low-loss paths
\cite{draxler2018essentially}. Solutions from the same initialization
are often \emph{linearly} mode connected.

\textbf{Bump analog (partial).} Two solutions fitting the same set of
bumps with slightly different parameters should be linearly connected.
Solutions fitting different subsets would not. The bump model lacks the
neuron-permutation symmetries that enrich mode connectivity in neural
networks.

\subsection{Outside scope}\label{sec:outside}

\paragraph{Grokking.} Delayed generalization long after memorization
\cite{power2022grokking} requires a mismatch between memorization and
generalization strategies, typically arising in discrete/algorithmic tasks.
The bump model's continuous regression structure does not support this.

\paragraph{Neural collapse, lottery tickets, loss of plasticity.}
These phenomena are specific to classification architectures (neural
collapse), network sparsity (lottery tickets), or sequential task
structure (plasticity loss), none of which have natural analogs in the
bump data-generating process.

\section{Earlier Annotated Bibliography}

\subsection*{Sharpness and Generalization}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\cite{hochreiter1997flat} Hochreiter and Schmidhuber (1997) ``Flat Minima'':} The seminal paper proposing that algorithms converging to broad, flat regions of the loss landscape generalize better. They argue that flat minima can be described with lower precision (linking to Minimum Description Length) and are more robust to perturbations in the weights and data.
    \item \textbf{\cite{keskar2017large} Keskar et al.\ (2017) ``On Large-Batch Training for Deep Learning'':} Empirically demonstrates the generalization gap between small-batch and large-batch SGD, concluding that large-batch training tends to get trapped in sharper minima, whereas the inherent noise of small-batch SGD drives the optimization towards flatter, more robust minima.
    \item \textbf{\cite{foret2021sharpnessaware} Foret et al.\ (2021) ``Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM)'':} Introduces an optimization procedure that explicitly seeks out flat minima by minimizing a worst-case loss within a local neighborhood (loss sharpness), rather than optimizing only the point estimate. This approach significantly improves generalization across various models.
    \item \textbf{\cite{cohen2021gradient} Cohen et al.\ (2021) ``Gradient Descent on Neural Networks Typically Occurs at the Edge of Stability'':} Analyzes the ``progressive sharpening'' phenomenon. The authors show that during training, the maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian increases until it reaches the theoretical stability limit $2/\eta$. After this point, the optimizer enters the ``Edge of Stability'' (EoS) regime, marked by oscillating, non-monotonic loss but continuous long-term convergence.
    \item \textbf{\cite{xie2020diffusion} Xie et al.\ (2021) ``A Diffusion Theory For Deep Learning Dynamics'':} Applies Kramers' escape rate and Freidlin-Wentzell theory to prove that SGD exponentially favors flat minima over sharp ones. This provides the physical mechanism for why noise ejects the optimizer from sharp, high-rank bumps early in training.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Convergence Rates for Low-Rank Structures}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\cite{chi2019nonconvex} Chi et al.\ (2019) ``Nonconvex Optimization Meets Low-Rank Matrix Factorization'':} Reviews the use of non-convex formulations (e.g., Burer-Monteiro) to solve high-dimensional low-rank problems. The authors demonstrate that SGD, with appropriate initialization, achieves nearly linear (exponential) convergence rates $O(\log(1/\epsilon))$. Crucially for our bump functions, these non-convex methods scale well in high dimensions, as their memory footprint and iteration complexity depend primarily on the intrinsic rank $r$ rather than the ambient dimension $d$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Adam and Effective Step Size}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\cite{kingma2014adam} Kingma and Ba (2014) ``Adam: A Method for Stochastic Optimization'':} The original paper introducing Adam. It establishes the interpretation of the Adam update step as being proportional to the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) of the gradients, where the moving average of the gradient acts as the signal and the uncentered variance acts as the noise.
    \item \textbf{\cite{orvieto2025adam} Orvieto and Gower (2025) ``In Search of Adam's Secret Sauce'':} A recent, comprehensive analysis showing that Adam's core advantage over simpler sign-based methods is its ability to establish an adaptive trust region. As the noise variance dominates the mean gradient, Adam implicitly shrinks its effective step size, automatically annealing the optimization process without requiring explicit global learning rate decay schedules.
\end{itemize}
